abstract: 'A cautionary tale about connectome-body models, in the spirit of the dead
  fish fMRI experiment. We constructed a digital chimera — a C. elegans worm connectome
  driving a MuJoCo biomechanical model of a fly body — and used deep reinforcement
  learning to train the interface. The resulting digital sphinx produces highly realistic
  fly walking kinematics and leg coordination patterns. Yet the model is biologically
  meaningless: worms move by wiggling, the fly brain is 500x larger than the worm''s,
  and fly legs alone are controlled by more motor neurons than exist in the entire
  worm nervous system. The worm connectome functions simply as a recurrent neural
  network with rich enough dynamics to support learning — it could be replaced by
  a randomly connected RNN with equal validity. The lesson: DRL is a capable optimization
  algorithm that will fit expressive networks to complex data even when imposed constraints
  are outright wrong. Behavioral fidelity is achievable without biological fidelity,
  making connectome-body models easy to overinterpret. Done carefully, virtual animals
  can be powerful partners to biological experiments, but only if their components
  and interfaces are grounded in biology.'
